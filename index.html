How big should our coverage survey be?

This tool finds the survey size where the cost of one more interview just stops being worth the better funding decision it buys. New here? Start with the plain-language guide. For the math, see the method notes and mapping to IDinsight's source script.

How this tool works — in plain English (or read the full guide)

The trade-off: a bigger survey costs more, but a bigger survey also tells you more, and better information leads to better funding decisions. This tool puts both sides in the same units — dollars — so you can see exactly where they cross. It recommends the largest survey at which the next batch of interviews still buys more decision-value than it costs. Below that size, you are leaving information on the table; above it, you are overpaying for precision you will not act on.

What you tell the tool

Your prior — what you'd guess the program's coverage to be without any survey. Enter a best guess plus a 95% range, or draw a custom shape if your beliefs aren't bell-shaped.

The cost-effectiveness math — how many people are eligible (caseload), how many would die untreated, how much treatment reduces that mortality, what GiveWell values a death averted at, and the two budgets at stake (scale-up vs. scale-down). The tool combines these into an implied coverage threshold p* above which scale-up wins, and per-unit-coverage values for each decision. Defaults are generic placeholders — swap in your program's numbers.

The survey logistics — cost per interview, optional fixed setup cost, and how aggressive you want to be (the default "8× ROI" means each marginal dollar of survey cost must still buy at least eight dollars of expected decision value).

What it computes

The tool simulates 20,000 alternate worlds drawn from your prior. In each world the true coverage is fixed but unknown. The tool simulates running the survey at sample size N, updates GiveWell's belief about coverage with the survey result, simulates the funding decision (scale up if the updated belief crosses p*; else scale down), and tracks the value that decision creates. Averaging across worlds gives the expected value of the survey at that N.

It repeats for many N values (100, 200, 300, …) and reports the largest N at which the next batch of interviews still clears your ROI hurdle. That's your recommended sample size.

Why Bayesian, not frequentist?

Frequentist power calculations target statistical precision: "how big does N need to be to reject a null hypothesis 80% of the time?" Bayesian VoI targets decision improvement: "how big does N need to be for the survey to actually shift (or confirm) GiveWell's funding call, enough to pay for itself?" The second question is closer to what we usually care about when writing M&E protocols.

What this tool is not

It's a design-stage sizing aid, not a full CEA. The simulation uses a simplified cost-effectiveness pipeline — one source of uncertainty (coverage), no auxiliary surveys, no caseload-inflation math. For the full IDinsight Nigeria CMAM setup, use the R script linked in the method notes.
